Governance: Conversations, Not Flowcharts

Governance is alignment over time built on trust — not a diagram, and increasingly something that can be encoded for machines to enforce.

Governance isn't a process artifact

Dan Mall's central claim: teams mistake governance for a flowchart, diagram, or checklist — but "governance isn't a flow." The teams that fail at it built a process without building trust: they wrote rules without buy-in, made a library no one used, published a flowchart no one followed.1 Good governance is alignment over time, and it only happens through understanding the current culture.

His practical reduction: governance answers who does what, and when? — discovered by talking to people about what they actually do, not what you wish they'd do, then written as a simple WHO/WHAT/WHEN list (e.g., "core team approves net-new components during weekly review; individual designers propose updates anytime via the tokens repo").1

The one-off vs. the missing piece

Frost frames the governance moment as a conversation: when a team wants to go outside the guidelines, determine whether the request is a one-off exception or a signal of a missing piece the system should absorb.2 This turns governance from gatekeeping into a feedback loop that grows the system.

Mall sharpens this with the example of a detached component: the instinct is to treat detachment as a discipline failure, but repeated detachment is usually information about a gap in the component API, variant set, or production context. Governance that punishes workarounds suppresses the feedback the system needs; governance that listens to workarounds improves with every cycle.4

Consistency is cultural before it is mechanical

Callahan reinforces the anti-magic view of systems: a design system alone will not make a product consistent, because teams can create inconsistent experiences inside even rigid systems. Consistency comes from culture — the shared expectations and decision filters that make people pause and ask whether work fits the product and brand.6

Systems as decision engines

Mall reframes the purpose itself: most teams treat design systems as collections of components, but the higher-leverage framing is the system as a decision engine — encoding decisions so they don't have to be re-litigated every time.1 Governance is how those decisions get made and maintained.

Governance under uncertainty

The measurement discussions add an important governance move: decisions can be provisional without being weak. Curtis describes aligning on the things already agreed, postponing the unresolved edges, and making "provisional decisions" for the middle so data can flow back into the system; Mall calls a similar move McDonald's Theory, where a deliberately imperfect proposal helps a group discover what it actually values.5 This reframes the design system as a pursuit of the current best answer rather than a source of perfect, unchanging truth.5

Encoding governance for agents

The agentic literature extends this: governance work is increasingly "encoded" as machine-readable rules agents must follow, letting design leaders guarantee certain decisions (e.g., "no new button styles") instead of relying on education alone.3 This is the bridge to Governance & Trust Levels for Agents — where the WHO/WHAT/WHEN list becomes enforceable policy.

Perspectives: trust-based vs. encoded governance

There is a productive tension here. Mall insists governance is fundamentally human and cultural — trust, not documentation.1 The agentic camp argues that some governance can and should be encoded as hard rules machines enforce.3 They aren't opposites: encoding works only for the decisions a team has already aligned on culturally. Encode the settled decisions; keep talking about the unsettled ones.
