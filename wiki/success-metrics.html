Success Metrics & the Value of a System

Consistency is table stakes, not the value proposition — the real case is the business problem the system solves.

Consistency is a bad top-line goal

Dan Mall's sharpest argument: if your system's top goal is "consistency," you're aiming too low. Consistency is a by-product, not a value proposition — the answer to "why are we investing in a design system?" can't be "so our buttons look the same."1 Better systems are built to unblock engineering bottlenecks, speed cross-functional workflows, empower junior contributors, and scale UX decisions across brands and markets.1

His homework assignment: replace "consistency" in your vision doc with a business problem the system actually solves — ideally one drawn from the words a VP or CTO uses at the all-hands. At one company, tying design-system work to "four key platforms" from the CTO's deck made people value the system more — and, ironically, the UIs became more consistent as a result.1

Efficiency numbers earn trust

The quantitative case is well documented: Uber's Base system let adopting teams ship 3× faster with 4× fewer visual bugs and half the UI code.2 These are the numbers a new team uses to earn internal credibility — without them, "you're just another initiative asking people to change how they work."

However, Itai Vonshak injects a critical economic caveat: full design system adoption is a highly expensive proposition. For most organizations, code reuse and design-code alignment are not enough to justify the sheer overhead of a dedicated team on cost savings alone.4 The math of efficiency only works if the system aligns with broader product quality and design excellence.

Leading indicators over lagging metrics

Mall pays attention to behavioral signals as leading indicators. His favorite: the moment a designer says "lemme just hop into Storybook and make the change myself." Roles converging, tools becoming shared, discipline lines blurring — that's the system becoming something owned in common, and it reliably precedes broader collaborative change.1

The measurement literature makes this more explicit: start from outcomes and OKRs, then measure signals such as adoption, coverage, dependency freshness, communication patterns, support themes, quality gaps, and stakeholder confidence. A system's value is realized when product teams ship with it, not when the library or docs site launches.67 See Measuring Design Systems.

Don't let savings vanish into throughput

A maturity trap Mall flags: when a system halves delivery time, sprint commitments often just double — the saved time disappears into throughput instead of going to maintenance, documentation, or quality. "Speed isn't the problem. Treating speed as the point is."1

Invisible work has no value story

Mall adds a second value trap: stakeholders evaluate what they have heard about, not everything the team actually shipped. A team that builds twelve components but communicates about three risks being judged as a team that built three; when budgets tighten, invisible output is easier to cut.5

Perspectives: is consistency the point?

This is one of the corpus's central debates, and the camps are closer than they appear.

  • Mall: consistency is table stakes / a by-product; the goal is the business problem solved.1
  • Zhang: consistency is the concrete Stage-1 deliverable that earns a new team its trust (the 3×/4× numbers are consistency numbers).2
  • Frost: reframes the whole frame — systems enable "flexibility, not uniformity"; good architecture lets brands look different while sharing a core.3
  • Vonshak: rejects consistency or rule adherence as a guarantee of quality (the "adherence fallacy"). Great apps are usable and desirable due to thoughtful design, not religious compliance with a rigid system.4

Synthesis: consistency is a necessary early proof point and a by-product of good architecture — but a poor north star. The durable goal is the business outcome (speed, scale, quality) that consistency quietly enables. See Token Architecture for the "flexibility, not uniformity" mechanism.
