Evals & Observability

Agentic workflows drift silently — so you benchmark them with eval harnesses and watch them with observability, the way you'd monitor any production system.

Why evals matter

Because agentic workflows can silently drift or degrade over time, leading teams invest in evaluation harnesses that exercise agents across many prompts and LLM backends, comparing generated components both in code and visually (Storybook snapshot tests, Figma diffing) to catch regressions before they reach production or design libraries.1 These proprietary test suites function as private evals—benchmarks testing models against specific business outcomes rather than generic public benchmarks. This builds a defensive data barrier that is essential to the firm's sovereign learning loop. (See Model Sovereignty & Token Capital).

Storybook Evals

Frost and team describe using Storybook Evals to benchmark a design system against test prompts — tracking performance over time, detecting drift, and understanding the trade-offs between speed, cost, and quality.2 Evals turn "does the agent do a good job?" from a vibe into a measured, repeatable signal. Atlassian's agentic roadmap similarly prioritizes the transition from template prompt-tuning to automated evaluations and benchmarking to programmatically verify design system compliance at scale (see Agentic Content & Prototyping Templates).

Broader agentic safety practices

Beyond evals, teams apply general safety practices: guardrails on allowed actions, logging and auditing of agent decisions, and circuit breakers for jailbreak-like prompts or out-of-scope tasks. Human-in-the-loop review stays central, especially for changes affecting governance, brand, or accessibility.1

Validation is half of readiness

Evals are the system-level expression of Frost's validation pillar (see Agentic Readiness): functional, accessibility, and visual tests are what give a team confidence in what agents assemble.2 Coverage tells the agent how to build; validation/evals tell you whether it did.

Perspectives: eval before you scale

The consistent recommendation is sequencing: build a small evaluation suite that benchmarks prompts, component coverage, and failure modes across LLMs before scaling agent usage, then expand autonomy as evidence accumulates.1 This pairs naturally with trust levels — evals are the evidence that justifies promoting an agent from "suggest only" toward "auto-merge" for a given action class.
